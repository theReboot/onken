After decades of total state control of media, campaigns for media reform intensified in the 1990s, effectively softening restrictive and punitive policies, and opening space for a commercialized media and expanded consumer choice.

Media’s liberalization had mixed effects. For one, local language FM radio stations mushroomed across the country and expanded access to information—playing a pivotal role in political and developmental mobilization efforts at the grassroots.

But, as media regulations loosened, political interests too took claim of radio frequencies and foreign entertainment flooded in—as it was cheaper than producing locally. Both of which have had long-lasting structural consequences for media ownership and investment in Kenya.

The Kenya Information and communication (Amendment) Act 2013 has given the state latitude to control the media landscape. Some of its provisions negate aspects of the 2010 Constitution on press freedom and freedom of the media.
